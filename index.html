9/5/24

My work within the Norwegian Correctional Service (NCS) took a big step forward last week, when I joined the correctional officer students at
KRUS to visit a Norwegian prison for the first time. Romerike Fengsel, (fengsel “fang-sill” is prison in Norwegian) is the largest prison
in Norway with a capacity of 286 people, and is a high-security facility for serious offenders. For reference, the Perryville prison in
Goodyear that I typically worked in at ASU has a capacity of 2300 people, which would make it just the 6th largest facility in Arizona.
Things are a lot different here.

That much was obvious even before walking through the front gate. For one, there was no dress code to be made aware of before the visit. No
bans on blue or red to avoid gang affiliation, no disallowing orange because it’s too close to the color of the prison jumpsuits, and no
preventing brown since it’s the color of the officer’s uniforms. Just plain clothes were all that were required to walk through the door -
the same the inmates themselves were wearing: their own clothes. The officers taking us around even let some of the students bring their
snus in along with them. A side note but snus, the European version of zyns, are way more popular than any sort of nicotine product I’ve
ever seen back in the US, and are shockingly common really anywhere you are in public. One of the other American Fulbrighters in Oslo even
mentioned one of their professors putting in a snu in while giving a lecture.

Within the prison, these differences persisted. The cells were much larger than their American counterparts, about the size of a dorm room,
with no cellmates and a TV at the end of each bed. Each cell also included its own toilet and shower, something I haven’t seen in any
prison I’ve been to. As mentioned before, each prison unit also comes with a communal kitchen, and also a shared living space with board
games and an xbox. Other features, like an inmate gym (much nicer than the one I’ve been using at KRUS) and soccer field are similarly
unheard of in the states. To finish off the tour, we were also shown the prison’s visitation rooms (including hotel ish rooms for
overnight visits that are allowed with partners) and also the officer’s training area - which doubles as a basketball court for the rest
of the prison when it isn’t being used.

Online picture of a Romerike cell Other online picture of a unit common area. Officer station can
be seen through the glass in the back

Even interactions between officers and inmates are far different than what I’m used to. Upon entering one unit, one of the students on the
tour - who was translating things to English for me since he had previously worked at that prison a few years prior - saw one of the
inmates he had worked with during his time at Romerike. Upon seeing each other, the two ran up and gave each other a big hug, before
joking and catching up for a minute. The officer guiding us joined in talking with the two, before allowing the inmate to take over the
tour for a minute to show us his area. Coming from a system where many older officers refuse to even shake the hand of any incarcerated
person, to call this a culture shock would be a massive understatement.

Despite the contrast, there still are a lot of asterisks that come with any prison, and especially the experience of touring one. Norway and
the NCS are incredibly impressive in their dedication to rehabilitation, but no amount of TVs and xboxes really distract from the fact
that you are in a prison. The experience of being separated from families, homes, and jobs is still omnipresent regardless of the country.
Tours themselves only show the aspects of a prison that officers would like to show - and although the officers of our tour were fairly
transparent about the use of more restrictive measures, particularly solitary confinement (something the NCS has been internationally
criticized of for their frequent use), there still always is another side. Prison tours themselves happen without the consent of any
person incarcerated, and while valuable in my experience to give context to what I read and hear about from inmates, there is not much of
a choice involved on their part with my being there.

All of this is to say there’s a lot to think about. My project focuses instead on people sentenced through at-home electronic monitoring,
rather than in a prison, but understanding the experiences of both are necessary to contextualize how to minimize the structural
difficulties people face at the end of their sentence. A lighter post about Scandinavian summer is coming soon, but thought a quick look
into what I’m working on was needed :)

8/31/24

A big week that’s already rushing to its conclusion! Tuesday and Wednesday I had my orientation for the Norway Fulbright program, my first
chance to meet the other Americans who will also be abroad this year. It was an exciting couple days, and busy ones too - in between lots
of introductions we also officially kicked off the year with a reception at the Nobel Institute, where the winner for the Peace Prize is
selected. There, we were welcomed by the US embassy and Norwegian Ministry of Education, and had the opportunity to share a little about
our projects. The event was fun, but more than anything it was comforting to meet people going through the similar experience of settling
into a new country. We were able to bond over both the excitement (where to visit, beer menus in Norway, etc.) and the struggles (the 6
hours I spent in a cramped San Francisco office for a ten minute residency appointment and similar horror stories) of the move so far, and
going forward it’s nice to know there’ll be plenty of friendly faces around the country to visit with couches to crash on!

The scene from the Nobel center The classiest part of the reception.. the box wine

To finish up our orientation, we were served lunch with an assortment of popular Norwegian foods. While I’ve already been asking people I
meet what a Norwegian diet consists of (among the answers frozen pizza, tacos, and pepsi max - devastating to diet coke drinkers
everywhere) this was my first chance to have a full spread of new things to try. One of the main components of our meal was different
ingredients for matpakke (maht-pahk-uh), which directly translates to “packed lunch” but usually entails an open-faced sandwich on
dense, whole-grain bread. Norwegians typically have four meals - breakfast, lunch, dinner around 5pm, and supper before bed - and these
types of meals are common for everything except dinner. Common toppings include shrimp with mayo (actually great), mackerel with tomato
(jury’s still out), smoked salmon, or any type of usual lunch meat and cheese. Other options for breakfast include jam or brown cheese - a
Norwegian delicacy I’ve been told is only eaten by grandparents but is a salty, caramel-like cheese that immediately gets stuck to the
roof of your mouth (and it’s fantastic). To top things off, we finished with a selection of Norwegian desserts, among my favorites the
Kvikk Lunsj (quick lunch), the Norwegian version of a Kit Kat.

Our matpakke topping selection And the infamous brown cheese

In one last note, with nearly every Norwegian I’ve met one of the first questions I immediately get is my thoughts on the upcoming
election. For a lot of Norwegians, American politics seem like a kind of perverse reality show that they follow with a true-crime like
fascination. It’s a difficult subject to broach, even in an intensely liberal country where my political beliefs mostly align with the
people I’m talking to. It feels like the easy way out to play the “cool” American who thinks other Americans are stupid and backwards
(despite how strongly I might disagree with what is happening sometimes), when instead there’s an opportunity for a more productive
conversation about values, the two-party system, and political representation or lack thereof. It’s a weird balance to strike while also
trying to show I’m empathetic and culturally-sensitive, and something I’m still figuring out.

Missing everyone in Arizona/off in new places!

8/24/24

Nothing like a couple mean 13 year olds to really inspire me to pick up the pace learning Norwegian! This week I revived my hockey career as
a coach, where I’ll be assisting with two teams, U12 and U13, at a local club this year. Hockey has been such a large part of my life for
a long time, and sharing the game in a country where it’s not huge - surprising, given Norwegians love every other winter sport - was one
of my biggest goals for this year.

In short, it’s been amazing so far. Stepping back on the ice was easy and natural, and each practice has flown by. But! As I’ve quickly found
out, there’s little filter, really ever with the kids, but particularly about my Norwegian accent (or lack thereof). When I’ve tried to
use Norwegian words or phrases around my roommates - like mentioning I wanted to go hiking in the nearby area of Akershus - I’ve mostly
gotten polite nods or corrections in pronunciation that usually end in a “close enough” after a few mostly futile attempts. With the kids,
no such luck. At my last practice, trying to learn the Norwegian words for blue and red (blå and rød), I asked two of the kids on the team
for help. After repeating the pronunciation back (blow and rue-uh, but obviously I’m not the authority here), they both looked at each
other and laughed. Humbling! A couple minutes later, I got a grimace from another kid when I asked what his name was in Norwegian (one of
very few phrases I felt confident about) before he told me my Norwegian didn’t make sense.

Very much standing out in the bright red warmies What the sign says!

On another note, it rains here! This is not remotely a surprise after even the most cursory Google search about the climate in Norway, but
I am quickly finding out the vast scientific consensus is no lie. I’m used to the type of rain in Arizona, where a storm is just some
quirky thing and rarely something that requires a rain jacket or umbrella. Caught in a storm? You might as well stand in it because it’s
the last you’re getting for a couple months. Here, you just end up already wet when you reenter the rain a couple hours later. To clarify,
I own a very capable rain jacket - I have all the resources at my disposal to avoid getting soaked. But, for whatever reason, when it
comes to walking out the door, my years of Arizona residency take over and I leave my apartment exposed to the elements. Anyways, this is
how I reached my hockey practice yesterday evening, running the walk from the metro stop to the rink, hockey bag slung over my shoulder
and avoiding the large puddles pooling on the mostly gravel path. Survival of the fittest and all that.

Otherwise, today marks one week in Norway! It feels like I’ve lived several lifetimes here already, but I’m excited for everything more that’s
still to come.

8/21/24

It’s a little past noon, and I’m sitting on the second floor of the Oslo public library. The crowd is mixed - students like myself
clustered around desks and outlets, kids from school groups in yellow crossing guard vests running outside of the watch of their teachers,
and tourists wandering in from the nearby museums to snap a couple pictures before braving the rain once again. It’s an impressive
building, even in a city that noticeably dedicates itself to public spaces. Sitting on the harbor, it’s an Oslo landmark along with
the nearby opera house (operahuset). But regardless of the place, it’s here that I've come to say I'm nearly a week into my stay in
Norway.

Oslo's operahouse (with a very walkable roof) With the public library next door on the left

The first few days have been a blur, a mix of ikea stops, tourist sights, and return trips to ikea after realizing that I still don’t actually
have bedsheets. This too has been while not knowing much of the language at all - something that's made little things like getting
groceries more difficult so far. The time I've spent looking blankly at different brands of greek yogurt is absurd. Fortunately, most
people speak good English - particularly close to the city center, which has made asking for help relatively easy. Even still, I'm trying
to pick up as much Norwegian as I can, though right now that's mostly limited to hei's (means hi, pronounced the same way) and unnskyld
med's (means excuse me, pronounced oon-shill may). It's a work in progress - though I've been assured that Norwegians sometimes can't even
understand other Norwegians because some regional dialects are so strong.

Yesterday was my first day at KRUS, the University College of the Norwegian Correctional Service, where I’ll be spending my year
researching Electronic Monitoring - essentially house arrest - in Norway. Already, the Norwegian approach to corrections is much different
than anything I’ve worked with before. Here, the training to become a correctional officer is a two-year program (and may become a three-
year bachelor's degree soon), where in addition to practical training students get classes in social work, sociology, philosophy, and
criminology. While security is still a primary focus in prison, there's also a much greater emphasis on job training, education, and
living in a way that prepares people for life after their release. For one, Norwegian prisoners have a shared kitchen and make their own food, with
access to kitchen tools up to and including knives.

On one last note, my room is finally starting to look like a place where someone lives! Last night I set up my cork board, something for me
that always makes a place a home. It was the first thing I set up in my Barrett dorm, and the last thing I took down moving out from my
apartment a couple weeks ago. I’m hoping to add a couple more stickers and postcards as I travel around here, but for now it’s nice to
have some reminders of home :)
