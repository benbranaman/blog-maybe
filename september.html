9/24/24

The first signs of fall are becoming all the more apparent in Oslo. Outside the city, the trees dotting the surrounding mountains have started to
take on red and orange tints, and at KRUS, conversations increasingly end with people going back and forth about which of their hometowns
have the worst winters. I’m no expert, but -20 Celsius with an ocean breeze and -30 inland sound equally unpleasant!! The day of reckoning is
soon to come where I find out whether the first real winter coat I’ve ever owned will be able to hold its own after all.

In the meantime, the biggest indication of the seasons changing has been in our dwindling hours of daylight. Just a few weeks ago, my before work
runs were in the sun, but now our sunrise isn’t until after 7am and rapidly is moving later. Same too with hockey, where I now exit to a dark
sky after an evening practice. With hockey itself, the season is entering full swing. It’s been nice to enter a routine as coach, and
wrangling the 12 and 13-year-olds on the teams I coach continues to be as fun and occasionally exasperating as ever. I’ve gotten to share
some of my own hockey traditions, like my old teams’ usual skating warmup, which we now start every practice with (“Arizona circles” as the
other coaches call it out to the kids). Some of the kids have also started to call me Ben Ken, I guess because the Barbie movie is their main
impression of America, which is 1) HIGHLY ironic in a country where every other person is blond, which I am very much not, and 2) feels like
a repeat of every time someone called me “Ben Ten” through elementary school. Other kids also continue to think I played college or minor
league hockey, which I also very much didn’t, but as much as I correct them otherwise I don’t mind the confusion.

Pregame huddle before my first game of the year on the bench Starting each game with a lightshow.. also unique to Norwegian hockey

With the game itself, the style of play is different than what I’m used to, in a way that sometimes reflects differences in cultural values
between here and the US. For one, the level of passing is beautiful compared to anything I’ve seen at a similar age, and their instincts for
including each other and finding the open player are way more advanced than what I’d expect. At the same time, there’s much less of a desire
to play physical and hit each other than I’m used to, particularly at their age where on my own teams over-eagerness was usually the issue
when checking was involved. While it’s probably better as far as safety’s concerned that I haven’t seen anyone get sent head first into the
boards, it’s definitely an indication of a different game being taught. Either way, it is still twelve and thirteen-year-old hockey, which in
any continent involves its fair share of mind-bogglingly awful offsides violations, lots of tears on the bench after someone has a hard fall,
and kids wildly over-estimating their own ability to hit a slap shot.

From Oslo's nasjonalmuseet, Monet's painting of Kolsåstoppen The actual mountain, just across the street from the rink!

This culture difference is especially felt on my younger team, where one of my players is significantly better than the rest of their teammates.
The other coaches constantly complain to me about them hogging the puck, and to be fair, they’re completely right. I’ve almost never seen
someone who looks like they’re physically having to restrain themselves from stealing the puck from their own teammates like this kid. But at
the same time, to me their talent is obvious and they’re oftentimes not being challenged by what’s going on at practice. When they are
challenged, too they’ve responded pretty well. While most of this is just low-stakes youth sports stuff, I see parallels in these differences
with the Norwegian idea of Janteloven. A set of ten informal social rules, Janteloven is often used as a general description for Norwegian
culture. Rule 1 goes “Do not think you are anything special”, Rule 7 is “Do not think you are good at anything”, and Rule 9 is an equally
cheery “Do not think anyone cares about you”. While these are obviously a little extreme, even in a Norwegian sense, they do demonstrate the
strong inclinations the country as a whole feels towards the value of equality. It’s this sort of approach that gives the country its
fantastic welfare system, and in my field, a strong lean towards rehabilitation rather than punishment in corrections. In most settings,
group cohesion is highly valued over individual success, to a degree that some critics of Norwegian culture feel that the sentiment falls
more on the side of sameness rather than equality. It’s an interesting dynamic, and a relevant issue in a country that through recent
immigration is experiencing more cultural heterogeneity than ever before.

In one more addition to my Norwegian hockey expertise, I’m going to my first Eliteserien (Elite Series - Norway’s top hockey league) game this
weekend! The team closest to me, Vålerenga, happens to be the proverbial Yankees of Norway hockey - with 30 championships but also three
straight playoff exits at the hands of their rivals Storhamar (literal translation stor “big” hamar “hammer” - cool.). With twenty-two years
of the gut-punch-that-kept-on-giving experience that was being a Coyotes hockey fan, I feel no guilt whatsoever about rooting for the
favorite for a change. Vålerenga’s stadium was built for the 1952 Oslo Winter Olympics, which at least shows that haven’t gotten kicked out
of their building for that long! Updates on the game soon to come.

9/17/24

Norwegian is a strange language. It’s at once relatively easy to read, since the grammar is pretty similar to English, but also very difficult
to pronounce and understand. It’s also a difficult language to learn at times, not because of the language itself, but because of many
Norwegian’s competency in English. I’m now two weeks into my weekly Norwegian class (norskkurs if you will), held at the University of Oslo
for international researchers, and am finding that others are facing the same dilemma. The class is an eclectic bunch, a group of 20 or so
that spans about 15 different countries of origin - and 6 different continents of origin at that. I’m the lone American representative.
While some of our makeshift UN is like me, having just arrived in the country (one month ago today to be exact!), others have been here
much longer, up to 10 years in some cases. Since many Norwegians are willing to switch to English while speaking so quick, you really can
get by that long if you want to. This fact was also echoed by some at the Fulbright office - if you want to learn, you have to make a
specific effort to speak and struggle through the language.

It’s with this in mind that I’ve been using my newly acquired library card so peruse the picture book section of the Oslo library to checkout
books like the riveting “Lars er Stor” (meaning “Lars is big”) - horrifying illustrations and all. No spoilers to those at home, but Lars
IS bigger than the baby but is NOT bigger than the mountain or the tree. I’m on the edge of my seat with every page.

Lars looks a little like the kid from that "No, David" picture
book... this is not a compliment

In a little bit of a history lesson, Norwegian also bizarrely has two different written forms: bokmål and nynorsk. Until 1814, Norway was under
Danish rule, and during that time spoke mostly Danish with a little bit of Viking-era Norwegian mixed in. However, after a bizarre treaty
in which Denmark essentially said “our bad” for being allied with France after Napoleon tried to invade most of Europe, they agreed to
leave Norway under Swedish control. With Danish rule over, many Norwegians saw the opportunity to establish a new, truly Norwegian,
official language. Most agreed that codifying how most people spoke (particularly in cities) - the Danish/old Norwegian blend - made the
most sense, and this would become bokmål. Today, this is the kind of Norwegian 90% of the population speaks, and is what I learn in my
class. However, one Norwegian named Ivan Aasen felt that cities were already too “corrupted by the Danish”. He undertook a genuinely insane
decade-plus long journey where he traveled from small village to small village in Norway, where he compiled all the local dialects he
encountered into a different version called nynorsk (ny, pronounced “knee”, means new so “New Norwegian”). This similar-ish language is
what 10% of people still speak today, most of those people being on the west coast where Aasen was from, and nynorsk is still a mandatory
subject in all Norwegian schools. However, in recent years, mass media has eroded the use of nynorsk, making its legacy an active area of
public debate.

All of this is to say that even if I somehow become coherent in bokmål someday, I have a whole entire other language still to learn.

Even when I know the right word to use, other cultural differences have still at times lead me astray. I mentioned in my first post I was
getting by with lots of “unnskyld”s, the Norwegian word for “excuse me” - at least, per Duolingo. However, I also learned that at times the
word means closer to the word sorry, but so does another word, “beklager”. I was asking my roommate about when to use which word when he
got confused about what I was asking… because Norwegians really just don’t say excuse me. They do, occasionally, but in a country that by
and large is extremely reluctant to talk to strangers, the inconvenience of having someone talk to you is considered way more rude than
anything you might have done to get in their way in the first place. So, by trying to use the language and keep out of people’s way, I
might have been outing myself as an overeager American this entire time.

Bear with me as I learn my æ’s, ø’s, å’s, and all the other faux pas I’ve unwittingly been committing.

9/11/24

In Norway, the working world comes to a shrieking halt on Sundays. Everything - grocery stores, your neighborhood Ikea, and the like, is
closed. Save for the library and a couple convenience stores here and there (with higher prices because workers must be paid overtime on
Sundays) - you’re left to your own devices for the day. Sometimes, this is inconvenient. My first full day in Norway was a Sunday, and
after having slept 15 hours the night before recovering from jet lag, I was ready to hit the ground running. No dice. If you didn’t get
groceries the night before, Sunday is a long day.

Otherwise though, Sundays are when the city comes alive. The first of my last couple of Sundays marked the beginning of one last heatwave
(peaking at 81 degrees - so a Phoenix Thanksgiving) to cap Oslo’s summer. And people were dead set on not missing their last few days of
warmth. My day started in Grünerløkka (grew-nehr-luck-uh), one of the trendier neighborhoods in the city, where on my way to meet a
fellow American Fulbrighter for coffee I was met with food stands, flea markets, and vintage clothing racks dotting the street. Literal
hundreds of people filled the sidewalks, in a way that felt befitting of some sort of oncoming parade, but it was just Sunday. Youth
sports teams sold home-baked cookies and pastries to fund their upcoming seasons, and school band and orchestra groups shuffled around
tables selling used winter and ski gear. Interestingly, across all stands, sales were conducted almost exclusively with Vipps, Norway’s
version of Venmo. Norway is an impressively cashless country, to the extent that a few years ago a bill was nearly passed to outlaw cash
altogether. Outside of the tip jar at said coffee place, I still have not seen a single Norwegian bill in my 3+ weeks here.

Coffee in hand, my day moved on to Hovedøya, the largest island in the fjord - the literal translation is hoved (main) øya (island), very
creative <3 - that lines the southern edge of Oslo. The island was a 10 minute ferry ride from the harbor, a journey that is part of the
city’s public transit system since some people commute to work by boat, something that feels very far from the many hours I’ve spent
agonizing around the I-10 I-17 interchange. On the short ride over, it was evident the crowds weren’t much different from what I saw in
the morning. Across the half dozen or so islands close to shore, dozens of families filled the landscape cruising on boats, swimming in
the water, lounging on beaches, or jumping from the rocks hanging over the bay. Hovedøya itself was packed with groups taking advantage of
its many walking trails (though after half a mile or so myself I lost most of the crowd) and grilling along its shorelines. It’s not just
in practice too that Norwegians take advantage of their natural surroundings. Allemannsretten, also known as the right to roam, legally
protects outdoor recreation on uncultivated land, even when that land is private property. These Sunday traditions are woven into the
fabric of Norwegian law.

Avoiding the crowds along the back of Hovedøya Another view of the Oslofjord, this time near the town of Drøbak

My day bookended with a morning run along Akerselva, the river that winds itself through the heart of Oslo, and a movie (Black Swan, which I
chose entirely because of the Childish Gambino lyric in Freaks & Geeks and had no idea what I was getting myself into), my full weekend
was complete. This emphasis on making the most of your weekends has been a special part of my time in Norway so far. It is never expected
to do work beyond or outside of work hours (some of this too is adjusting to working and not having homework), and Norwegians in general
are very protective of their work-life balance. This identity even takes shape in basic questions: while Americans might respond to “What
do you do?” with their job, Norwegians might be more likely to respond to the question with some of their hobbies. In short, it makes for
a very pleasant recharge before the work week starts up. I love my Scandinavian Sundays.

9/5/24

My work within the Norwegian Correctional Service (NCS) took a big step forward last week, when I joined the correctional officer students at
KRUS to visit a Norwegian prison for the first time. Romerike Fengsel, (fengsel “fang-sill” is prison in Norwegian) is the largest prison
in Norway with a capacity of 286 people, and is a high-security facility for serious offenders. For reference, the Perryville prison in
Goodyear that I typically worked in at ASU has a capacity of 2300 people, which would make it just the 6th largest facility in Arizona.
Things are a lot different here.

That much was obvious even before walking through the front gate. For one, there was no dress code to be made aware of before the visit. No
bans on blue or red to avoid gang affiliation, no disallowing orange because it’s too close to the color of the prison jumpsuits, and no
preventing brown since it’s the color of the officer’s uniforms. Just plain clothes were all that were required to walk through the door -
the same the inmates themselves were wearing: their own clothes. The officers taking us around even let some of the students bring their
snus in along with them. A side note but snus, the European version of zyns, are way more popular than any sort of nicotine product I’ve
ever seen back in the US, and are shockingly common really anywhere you are in public. One of the other American Fulbrighters in Oslo even
mentioned one of their professors putting in a snu in while giving a lecture.

Within the prison, these differences persisted. The cells were much larger than their American counterparts, about the size of a dorm room,
with no cellmates and a TV at the end of each bed. Each cell also included its own toilet and shower, something I haven’t seen in any
prison I’ve been to. As mentioned before, each prison unit also comes with a communal kitchen, and also a shared living space with board
games and an xbox. Other features, like an inmate gym (much nicer than the one I’ve been using at KRUS) and soccer field are similarly
unheard of in the states. To finish off the tour, we were also shown the prison’s visitation rooms (including hotel ish rooms for
overnight visits that are allowed with partners) and also the officer’s training area - which doubles as a basketball court for the rest
of the prison when it isn’t being used.

Online picture of a Romerike cell Other online picture of a unit common area. Officer station can
be seen through the glass in the back

Even interactions between officers and inmates are far different than what I’m used to. Upon entering one unit, one of the students on the
tour - who was translating things to English for me since he had previously worked at that prison a few years prior - saw one of the
inmates he had worked with during his time at Romerike. Upon seeing each other, the two ran up and gave each other a big hug, before
joking and catching up for a minute. The officer guiding us joined in talking with the two, before allowing the inmate to take over the
tour for a minute to show us his area. Coming from a system where many older officers refuse to even shake the hand of any incarcerated
person, to call this a culture shock would be a massive understatement.

Despite the contrast, there still are a lot of asterisks that come with any prison, and especially the experience of touring one. Norway and
the NCS are incredibly impressive in their dedication to rehabilitation, but no amount of TVs and xboxes really distract from the fact
that you are in a prison. The experience of being separated from families, homes, and jobs is still omnipresent regardless of the country.
Tours themselves only show the aspects of a prison that officers would like to show - and although the officers of our tour were fairly
transparent about the use of more restrictive measures, particularly solitary confinement (something the NCS has been internationally
criticized of for their frequent use), there still always is another side. Prison tours themselves happen without the consent of any
person incarcerated, and while valuable in my experience to give context to what I read and hear about from inmates, there is not much of
a choice involved on their part with my being there.

All of this is to say there’s a lot to think about. My project focuses instead on people sentenced through at-home electronic monitoring,
rather than in a prison, but understanding the experiences of both are necessary to contextualize how to minimize the structural
difficulties people face at the end of their sentence. A lighter post about Scandinavian summer is coming soon, but thought a quick look
into what I’m working on was needed :)
